Few policies have signaled as profound a reform as Executive Order (EO-14191), [Expanding Educational Freedom and Opportunity for Families](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/02/03/2025-02233/expanding-educational-freedom-and-opportunity-for-families?utm_campaign=the-economics-of-bulls-books&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=www.novelnewsletter.com) (EEF). Signed on January 29, 2025 under the Trump administration, EEF aims to infuse US education with, perceived, market-driven efficiency. By reallocating federal funding toward school choice initiatives—including vouchers, charter schools, and private faith-based institutions—EEF seeks to invigorate the education sector with market fundamentals.

At its core, EEF is both an ideological statement and an economic disruption. Advocates hail it as a necessary correction to a stagnant public education monopoly, while detractors warn of financial instability. The economic implications for investors, entrepreneurs, and policymakers are vast, creating opportunities in education-adjacent markets from EdTech to real estate.

“An investment in knowledge pays the best interest.” — Benjamin Franklin

EEF isn’t just about school choice—it represents a significant restructuring in the distribution of federal education funds. While the goal is to expand options for families, the real impact lies in the numbers: $10–20 billion is being redirected from long-standing programs like _Title I_, _IDEA_, and the _Child Care Development Block Grant_ into a new funding model. Its provisions reflect policy proposals which envision a decentralized, market-oriented education model.

The Heritage Foundation, a prominent conservative think tank, has been instrumental in shaping the policy framework through its "Project 2025" blueprint. Faith-Based groups, parental rights organizations, The 1776 Commission, charter school advocacy networks, veteran associations, and Republican legislators support the initiative to redirect federal education funds toward school choice programs. Lawmakers and allied federal policy makers seek to codify many of its provisions into law.

- _Senators Tim Scott (R-SC) and Bill Cassidy (R-LA) —_ co-sponsors of the Education Freedom Scholarships and Opportunity Act, a bill proposing a $10 billion annual allocation for private school scholarships.
    
- _Congressional School Choice Caucus —_ GOP lawmakers who collaborate with advocacy organizations like EdChoice and the American Federation for Children to promote expanded voucher programs and Education Savings Account (ESA) initiatives.
    
- _Legislative Endorsements_ — Republican legislators and their co-sponsored bills further corroborates the influence of these groups.
    

A coalition of twelve states, including Arizona, Florida, and Ohio, have implemented universal or near-universal school choice programs. Meaning, EEF shouldn’t be seen as a momentary policy turn but as part of a broader historical continuum.

- _The GI Bill (__1944)_ — redefined the trajectory of millions, opening doors to higher learning that had once been bolted shut. In its wake, a new middle class emerged.
    
- _Charter School Expansion (1990s–2000s)_ — in 1991, Minnesota etched its name into history, pioneering the first charter school law and setting the stage for an educational model driven by choice and competition.
    
- _Federal Measures_ (2017–2020) — President Trump’s first term saw more than $1.5 billion channelled into charter school expansion. Simultaneously, 529 plans—once exclusive to college tuition—were broadened to cover K-12 education.
    

EEF is another stitch in the pattern, altering American education in ways both profound and contentious. There are several key beneficiaries—some expected, others less obvious. 

- _Private & Faith-Based Schools: A Renaissance in Enrollment_ —  
    With federal funds now supplementing voucher programs, private and faith-based institutions are primed for enrollment surges. Arizona’s universal voucher program experienced a 574% increase in enrollment after its universal expansion.
    
- _EdTech & Curriculum Providers: The New Architects of Learning_ —  
    Anticipated spikes in demand for online learning platforms, EdTech firms and specialized publishers are crafting materials that align with revised cultural priorities.
    
- _Charter School Operators: Expansion Without Restraint_ —  
    Federal grants and deregulation create favorable conditions for expansion, particularly in states with conservative leadership, such as Texas and Florida.

By 2030, the alternative education market is projected to soar past $100 billion. Growth isn’t tied to the success of any one school but rather to the overall expansion of private education. Companies that provide infrastructure, technology, and operational support are set to gain just as much—if not more—than direct education providers.

- Stride Inc. (LRN) _—_ saw their stock price increase 7% on the same day as the EEF announcement. A coincidence, perhaps.
    
- _Cloud Campus ERP —_ offers features that streamline fee management with efficient fee voucher generation and management. It also engages parents through LMS Parent features for transparent fee communication and management.
    
- _American Heritage Education (AHE)_ — stands to benefit significantly from school vouchers, particularly in states embracing universal choice policies and Education Savings Accounts (ESAs). Due to their classical, faith-based, and patriotism curricula.
    
- _Education Savings Account (ESA) vouchers —_ provide public funds that can be used not only for tuition but also for other educational expenses, such as transportation. In Mississippi, the ESA voucher program allows funds to be used for transportation costs.
    

The biggest challenge will be navigating the complex web of regulations and compliance requirements, particularly those tied to government funding and educational standards. Regulations vary from state to state, making compliance even more cumbersome for businesses operating across multiple locations.

EEF is not about injecting new money into the system; it’s about redirecting existing funds. Grants serve as a primary mechanism for strategically redirecting federal funding toward school-choice initiatives. Grant options extend beyond the Department of Education. The order specifically calls on agencies like Health and Human Services and the Bureau of Indian Education to realign their grants with this school choice focus.

- _Mandating Education Freedom in Grant Allocation —_ EEF requires the Secretary of Education to embed "education freedom" as a central criterion in discretionary grant programs. This directive ensures that proposals emphasizing school choice, educational innovation, and competition with traditional public schools receive priority consideration. 
    
- _Interagency Review and Strategic Realignment —_ within 90 days of enactment, the Secretaries of Education and Labor must conduct a thorough review of existing discretionary grant programs. Their findings, submitted to the Assistant to the President for Domestic Policy, will include strategic plans for reallocating funds to support educational alternatives. 
    
- _Support for Low-Income and Working Families (Section 5) —_ beyond discretionary grants, EEF opens additional funding pathways. The Secretary of Health and Human Services is tasked with issuing guidance within 90 days on leveraging block grants—such as the Child Care and Development Block Grant—to finance educational alternatives. Based on publicly available data, the Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG) program has historically received roughly $4 to $6 billion in annual appropriations—in recent years, about $5 billion per year.
    
- _Assistance for Military Families (Section 6) and Bureau of Indian Education (BIE) Schools (Section 7) —_ the Secretaries of Defense and the Interior must evaluate existing funding methods, including grant-like structures, to enhance school choice options. Each department must submit a detailed implementation plan for the 2025–26 school year.
    

While specific estimates vary among analysts, some reports suggest that over $10–20 billion in annual federal funds could eventually be moved under the umbrella of educational choice initiatives.

As federal agencies craft new solicitations, competitively awarded contracts will emerge from a redirected funding pool. However, the precise dollar amounts allocated to these contracts remain undefined. While specifics regarding funding ceilings and contract structures unfold through agency guidance and appropriations, [USAspending.gov](https://usaspending.gov/?utm_campaign=the-economics-of-bulls-books&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=www.novelnewsletter.com) will serve as the hub for monitoring how federal dollars are deployed.

While established organizations and charter networks are clear winners, some compelling options are concealed in the niches. Online vocational courses, apprenticeships, and industry-backed certification programs offer flexibility while ensuring students develop skills that align with workforce demands.

Live, interactive classes with subject-matter experts further enhance this tailored educational experience, making learning more dynamic and personalized. A promising innovation in this space is a Netflix-for-education model, where families subscribe to a platform that provides access to curated courses, flexible learning paths, and interactive assessments with progress tracking. This format allows parents to handpick educational content.

As the demand for compliance increases, workforce training, values-based education, tailored legal, HR, and specialized curricula grows. Industry partnerships, and certification programs present valuable opportunities for innovation and expansion.

# Criticisms & Risks

The Education Trust (EdTrust) has raised concerns that voucher programs may contribute to worsened academic outcomes for low-income students, exacerbating existing disparities. In states where vouchers have been expanded to all families—regardless of income or demonstrated need—the primary beneficiaries tend to be those who were never enrolled in public schools.

- In Arkansas, 95% of students using vouchers in recent years did not transfer from public schools.
    
- In Arizona, 71% of students using Education Savings Accounts (ESAs) in 2023 were already enrolled in private or homeschool settings before receiving public funds.
    

These figures prompt a crucial question: Are voucher programs genuinely expanding access for underserved students, or are they merely subsidizing choices already made by wealthier families? Moreover, fiscal instability and inevitable legal challenges present significant factors that cannot be overlooked.

- _Public School Funding Risks_ – Redirecting over $10 billion in federal grants toward private education could exacerbate financial shortfalls in already struggling public school districts, particularly those serving lower-income populations.
    
- _Accountability Gaps_ – Arizona’s voucher program came under scrutiny when funds were misused for non-educational purchases, highlighting the need for stricter oversight and transparency in spending.
    
- Market Competition – As more companies and schools enter the private education sector, increased competition could lead to compressed profit margins and inconsistencies in quality.
    
- Scalability Challenges – Not all private schools and EdTech companies are prepared to absorb the surge in demand, raising concerns about capacity, staffing, and infrastructure.
    
- _Blaine Amendments_ – Several state constitutions, including Florida’s, prohibit public funds from supporting religious education. Legal battles over these provisions could complicate the expansion of school choice initiatives.
    
- _Federal Overreach_ – Some conservative lawmakers argue that directing federal funds toward private education could undermine state autonomy, leading to resistance from those who view education policy as a state-level responsibility.
    
- Regulatory Uncertainty – Future administrations or court rulings could alter, restrict, or even reverse current funding allocations.
    

Public scrutiny is inherent with an executive order as controversial and widely debated. Thriving in this environment requires a strong understanding of the education system, the politics, and public sentiment.

- Partner with private school chains and ESA management firms in states with established voucher programs.
    
- Monitor discretionary grant allocations, particularly those aimed at military and low-income family education subsidies.
    
- Develop niche services in emerging markets like DEI compliance audits, patriotic curriculum design, and voucher management software.
    

We’ll soon see whether education flourishes through competition or falters beneath its burden. Whether public or private, choice alone does not ensure success—effective execution does. Those who act with purpose, remaining informed and engaged, will shape what lies ahead.
